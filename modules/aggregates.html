Aggregate Functions, GROUP BY & HAVING

Aggregate functions collapse many rows into a single value — a count, a total, an average. GROUP BY runs those aggregates per group instead of over the whole table, and HAVING filters the groups afterwards. Together they answer almost every "per category" question you'll ever ask of data.

WHERE vs HAVING: WHERE filters rows before grouping; HAVING filters groups after aggregation. You cannot use an aggregate in WHERE — that's exactly what HAVING is for.

5Pitfalls & exam tips

⚠️ Common mistakes

  • Selecting a non-aggregated column that isn't in GROUP BY — illegal in standard SQL (and ambiguous).
  • Putting an aggregate in WHERE (e.g. WHERE AVG(salary)>5000) — use HAVING.
  • Forgetting that COUNT(col) skips NULLs while COUNT(*) doesn't.
  • Assuming AVG divides by total rows — it divides by the count of non-NULL values.

💡 Exam tips

  • Logical execution order: FROM → WHERE → GROUP BY → HAVING → SELECT → ORDER BY.
  • Every column in SELECT must be either aggregated or listed in GROUP BY.
  • HAVING without GROUP BY treats the whole table as one group.
  • To filter rows and groups, combine WHERE (rows) with HAVING (groups).
